# Interview Capture — Field-by-Field Guidance

Good interview notes are mostly verbatim quotes and observed behaviour. Interpretation comes later. The six fields exist to separate what the user said, what they did, and what you think it means — so later synthesis isn't polluted by in-the-moment bias.

## 1. Participant

Capture:
- **ID/pseudonym** — P1, P2, etc. Never store real names or emails; anonymise by default.
- **Role / background** — "home cook, three kids, cooks 5 nights/week" or "product manager at a 200-person SaaS company."
- **Recruitment channel** — "beta list", "cold outreach via LinkedIn", "existing user contacted via in-app prompt."
- **Current alternative** — what they use today to do the job your product is targeting. "Uses a paper notebook and Pinterest."

Why: downstream analysis needs to know who you talked to. A pattern that holds across "3 home cooks" is different from one that holds across "3 PMs evaluating B2B tools."

## 2. Key quotes

Capture verbatim, not paraphrased. If the user told you something surprising or captured the problem succinctly, record their actual words — in quotation marks.

Good:
> "I always end up cooking the same three things because the list of options is too overwhelming."

Bad:
> User reports feeling overwhelmed by the recipe list.

The paraphrase is an interpretation. The quote is a fact. Later, when you're synthesising, you'll want the quote.

## 3. Observations

What you *saw them do*, separate from what they *said*. Behaviour often contradicts reported preference.

Examples:
- "Said she wanted more recipes, but scrolled past 40 recipes before clicking one."
- "Said the app was 'fine', but hesitated 8 seconds before every tap in the onboarding."
- "Claimed to cook daily but the fridge camera showed empty shelves."

Observations are the difference between surveys (self-reported) and interviews (watch what happens). Capture them deliberately.

## 4. Pain points

Frustrations the user explicitly named. These are easy to collect but often surface-level — the real pain usually shows up in observations, not in what the user volunteers.

Examples:
- "Can't filter by cook time."
- "Doesn't trust the auto-generated shopping list."
- "Had to re-enter diet preferences on the web after setting them on mobile."

## 5. Goals / desires

What the user was trying to accomplish, in their own words. Bigger than pain points — a goal is the outcome they wanted, not the obstacle they hit.

Examples:
- "Cook something new every week without spending an hour planning."
- "Find one recipe the kids will eat and I won't mind making."
- "Feel organised about the week by Sunday night."

## 6. Follow-up questions

What you wish you'd asked. What the next interview should probe. These are the seeds of the next research session.

Examples:
- "Ask next: do they use a shopping app today? What?"
- "Probe whether 'overwhelming' means too many options or the wrong options."
- "Find out if they've tried competing products — which and why did they stop?"

## One last rule: don't merge interviews

Each interview gets its own section. Don't combine two participants' answers into one set of bullets — you'll lose the ability to see which patterns held for whom during synthesis.
